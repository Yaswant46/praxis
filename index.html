The Methodology

What is Scenario Planning?

Scenario planning is not forecasting. Forecasting assumes the future is a continuation of the present. Scenario planning assumes it is not.

It is a discipline of structured imagination — building internally consistent pictures of possible futures, then making decisions that are robust across all of them. Originally developed at Shell Oil in the 1970s to navigate the energy crisis, it has since become a core tool of strategic leadership at the highest levels of business and government.

The skill it builds is not prediction. It is the ability to hold multiple futures in your mind simultaneously, reason about their implications, and make commitments anyway.

"The real purpose of scenario planning is not to predict the future but to free yourself from an over-attachment to one particular version of it."

Pierre Wack — Shell Group Planning, 1985
Principle 01
Decisions compound across time
A choice made in Q1 does not resolve in Q1. It creates the conditions that constrain what is possible in Q4. Scenario planning makes these chains of consequence visible before they are irreversible.
Principle 02
Ambiguity is the operating condition, not the exception
The question is never "what will happen?" It is "what do we do when we cannot know?" Leaders who mistake uncertainty for a solvable problem underperform those who make peace with it.
Principle 03
The function boundary is a liability, not a feature
Every major business decision crosses at least three functions. Leaders who reason from inside a single domain make locally rational but globally destructive choices. Integration is the skill.
The Platform

How Praxis Works

Six leadership boards. The same company. Parallel realities. One day to find out whose decisions hold.

Before01
The Case
Each cohort receives a Harvard-style business case — a real EV startup or product company at a crossroads. Detailed financials, competitive landscape, and five strategic challenges. You read it the morning of.
During02
Five Quarters
Your team makes decisions across five domains every round — product, operations, finance, commercial, and people. Each decision is structured, every rationale is written. The Game Master injects live curveball events mid-round.
After03
The Debrief
All six boards' decisions are anonymised and compared. The debrief is not about who won — it is about which reasoning held up, which assumptions proved wrong, and what Q1 decisions created in Q4.
Scoring04
Leadership Index
Teams are scored across four dimensions: Financial Health (30%), Market Position (25%), Organisational Resilience (25%), and Innovation Pipeline (20%). The formula is never shown.
Reflection05
Considerations
After each round, every team receives probing questions based on their specific decisions. Not "you should have done this" — but "have you thought about x while making this decision?"
Memory06
Persistent Record
Every decision, rationale, and outcome is stored permanently. The Game Master can generate a full decision analysis report across all teams — a record of the cohort's reasoning that lives beyond the day.

Design Principles

What Praxis is built on.

01
Ambiguity lives in consequence, not in the questionEvery decision in Praxis feels clear. What participants cannot yet see is how their answer reverberates three quarters later.
02
Rationale matters more than choiceEvery domain requires written reasoning. A wrong decision made for good reasons is more developable than a right decision made by instinct.
03
Parallel realities, not competitionSix teams run the same company. The debrief is not about who won — it is about what different reasoning produced from identical starting conditions.
04
Feedback is probing, not prescriptivePost-round assessments ask "have you considered x?" not "you should have done y." The goal is reflection, not correction.
